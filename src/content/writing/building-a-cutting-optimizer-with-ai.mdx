# {frontmatter.title}

I love building stuff. Not the "launch a SaaS" kind. The other kind. Hands dirty, sawdust everywhere. A project where the bugs are literally splinters.

So I'm back in woodworking mode, building shelves for the HVAC room. You know, the kind of thing nobody praises you for, but you feel weirdly proud of.

And then came "the moment." You're staring at a sheet of plywood and doing mental math because that sheet isn't cheap. One wrong measurement and you're not iterating, you're just burning money.

## The problem

I had standard plates (2400mm by 1200mm), a list of shelf pieces, and zero desire to waste wood. I needed a tool to tell me how to cut efficiently.

What I found online: enterprise software with "talk to sales" pricing, ad-infested mobile apps, and one tool that looked like Windows 95.

I'm not paying €200 per month to cut shelves for a room that stores the vacuum cleaner.

## The dev brain kicks in

I could already picture it: a Piece type, a Sheet, a packing algorithm, a little renderer. Suddenly I'm halfway into a weekend project that has nothing to do with shelves.

I've been down this road before: Node.js dependency tangles, canvas libraries breaking, you know the vibe. But this time I didn't want to build it "properly." I just wanted the shelves.

## So I used AI. Actually used it.

AI worked best when I treated it like a fast junior dev, not a magical oracle. Clear requirements in, careful review out.

I used Claude Code with z.ai's GLM 4.7 model and went super concrete:

1. Here are the plate dimensions
2. Here are the pieces with width, height, and quantity
3. Output needs to match real world constraints
4. Give me a visual plan
5. Optimize for practical cuts, long straight ones

Because an "optimal" layout requiring a hundred weird cuts isn't optimal. It's punishment.

## Minutes later, I had a tool

Not production-ready. No auth, no database, nothing that should touch the public internet. And that's the point.

It was disposable software. A micro tool that lives just long enough to save your Saturday. It took my pieces, did the math, and drew a plan I could actually follow. Waste areas highlighted. Pieces visible. Layout matched reality.

I didn't implement bin packing from scratch. But I did get a better result than what I would've shipped in a rushed afternoon.

The iteration loop was tight: generate, stare, complain, regenerate.

"Too many weird cuts."
"Optimize for long straight cuts."
"Account for blade width."

Same as product engineering, just without the sprint planning meeting.

## This is just internal tooling for your life

You already hack scripts to clean up datasets or build dashboards to keep systems honest. Now you can do the same for physical-world problems.

I'm not saying you should never write algorithms yourself. But if the goal is the shelf, not the solver...

Use the solver. Delete it later. Move on.

The part I like most is practical: less waste, fewer recuts, fewer extra trips. Small efficiency wins, but real ones.
