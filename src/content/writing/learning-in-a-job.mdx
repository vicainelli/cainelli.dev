# {frontmatter.title}

> "Do you have experience with Rust?"
> "Not yet, but I can definitely figure it out."

If you've ever worked in tech, you've probably been in that situation. You're asked about a language, a framework, a tool, or system you've never touched, and instead of saying no, you say, give me a minute.

That's what working in tech really is. The ability to learn in real time. To take everything you've already learned, technical or not, and stack it together into something new that somehow works.

Because let's be real, even when we look like we know what we're doing, we're still figuring things out as we go. Every new challenge is a remix of what we already know, built on top of layers and layers of past experiments, mistakes, and discoveries.

It's this weird blend of chaos and creativity. One moment you're deep into debugging a production issue, and the next you're reading the documentation for some tool that didn't exist last month. You constantly jump between understanding how something works and inventing a way to make it work for you.

Over time, you start to realize that your real "skill" isn't just coding or knowing the latest stack. It's the confidence that you'll be able to learn whatever comes next. It's that quiet trust in your process of discovery. And that is the gist of working with technology. We're always building new things, and still learning how to build them.

## The skill that actually matters

Here's something I've noticed recently. The tech industry in 2025 is placing unprecedented value on adaptability and learning agility. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report identifies curiosity, lifelong learning, and technological literacy as core skills that complement the top technical abilities.

What does this mean in practice? It means the ability to continuously learn on the job, embrace change, and rapidly acquire new skills is becoming more valuable than any specific technical skill, which may quickly become outdated.

Companies are recognizing adaptability as the "ultimate differentiator" in hiring decisions. It's the fastest growing, in demand skill that employers look for. Robert Walters reports that flexibility, resilience, and a willingness to embrace change define success in modern tech roles.

## Learning agility is the new technical skill

Adaptability in technology encompasses more than just learning new tools. It's about being open to different approaches, with flexibility being key. The top transferable skills for tech professionals in 2025 include learning mindset, problem solving skills, and the ability to adopt change.

Learning agility, digital literacy, and curiosity are critical for building a successful tech career. The mindset focused on innovation, problem solving, and critical thinking, along with the ability to respond quickly to changes, is what future proofs your career.

This matters because specific technologies come and go. But the ability to figure things out, that stays with you.

## How to actually develop this

One of the best ways to build learning agility is to accept projects that require skills beyond your current set. This lets you learn new things and collaborate with others who have different expertise.

It's not about being the expert in everything. It's about being comfortable not knowing, and having the confidence that you can figure it out.

The tech professionals who thrive are the ones who approach each new challenge with curiosity rather than fear. They see unfamiliar technologies as opportunities to learn, not threats to their competence.

## Trust your process

So the next time someone asks if you know a technology you've never touched, remember this. Your real value isn't the stack you already know. It's your ability to learn the next one.

The confidence that you'll figure it out. That quiet trust in your process of discovery. That's what makes you valuable.

And honestly? That's the best part of working in tech. We get paid to learn, experiment, and build things that didn't exist yesterday. We're always beginners at something.

The trick is embracing it.
